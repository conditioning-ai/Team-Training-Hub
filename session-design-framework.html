Designing Game-Like Learning Environments That Connect to Match Demands

A framework for building sessions that develop players as decision-makers, not just movement performers. Every session follows a clear learning thread linking decisions, challenge, and game realism.

Session Principles
Attention  |  Intent  |  Intensity
Every Session Follows
A clear learning thread linking Decisions, Challenge, and Game Realism. The loop is not linear — each phase feeds back into the next. Practices connect to match performance.
Phase 01 · Arrival / Priming
Arrival & Priming
  • Individual or small physical & technical preparation
  • Player-led — driven by the leadership group
  • Strategic & tactical discussion and walkthroughs
  • Session theme activated from minute one
Phase 02 · Athletic Prep
Athletic Prep & Development
  • Body prepared. Robustness built. Ball included.
  • Plyometrics, bounds, sprints — weekly staple
  • Physical battles, competitive drills at intensity
  • Linked to session tactical theme where possible
Phase 03 · Directional Games
Directional Conditioned Games
  • Game-based learning with direction and scoring zones
  • Constraints, rules, space & numbers manipulation
  • Tactical + physical demands layered simultaneously
Phase 04 · Tactical Game
Tactical Conditioned Game
  • Full or modified game — game-realistic conditions
  • Theme expressed through player roles & principles
  • Realistic decision-making environment
⚡ Approach
Constraints-Led Approach
The Constraints-Led Approach (CLA) is rooted in Newell’s model of constraints, which proposes that movement behaviour emerges from the interaction of three categories of constraints: the individual (player abilities, perception, decisions), the task (rules, goals, equipment), and the environment (space, surface, weather, numbers).

The coach’s role in CLA is not to prescribe movement, but to design the environment and manipulate the task so that the desired behaviour emerges naturally. Players are not told what to do — the constraints invite the movement or decision the coach wants to develop.

In practical terms: reducing the space forces quicker decisions; overloads create attacking opportunities; limiting touches forces off-ball movement; removing the keeper creates goal-scoring urgency. Change the constraint, change the behaviour.
Constraint Type 01
Task Constraints
Rules, scoring systems, number of touches, time limits, ball restrictions. Most directly controllable by the coach in session design.
Constraint Type 02
Environmental Constraints
Size and shape of space, number of players, goals, cones, surfaces. Creating overloads, underloads, or zones that invite specific decisions.
Constraint Type 03
Individual Constraints
Physical capacity, technical ability, decision-making speed, experience. The coach adapts task and environment constraints to meet where the individual is.
Coach Role
Designer, Not Director
The coach creates the problem. The player solves it. Intervene less, design better. Freeze the game only when the constraint has failed to produce the desired behaviour.
💡 Methodology
Differential Learning
Differential Learning (DL) was developed by sports scientist Wolfgang Schollhorn and challenges the traditional assumption that skill is acquired through repetition of an ideal movement pattern. In DL, variability is the method — no two repetitions are the same.

Where traditional practice tries to eliminate errors and reduce noise, DL deliberately introduces fluctuations into every rep. Different heights, weights, angles, speeds, distances, and starting positions on every repetition. The player never gets comfortable — and that discomfort is the learning.

The neuroscientific basis is that the brain’s motor system develops robustness through exposure to a wide solution space, not through grooved repetition. Players who train with DL are better able to adapt in match conditions because they have encountered — and solved — a far wider range of movement problems in training.

Practical rule: the coach varies the return ball on every single rep. No two balls the same. Players must read and adapt each time. Over-coaching kills the process — let the variability do the teaching.
Principle 01
No Fixed Model
There is no ‘perfect’ technique to imitate. The player builds their own optimal movement solution through exploration of varied stimuli.
Principle 02
Variability = Stimulus
Every rep is different. Height, weight, angle, timing, distance all vary. The difference between reps is where the learning lives.
Principle 03
Self-Organisation
Players organise their own movement solutions. The coach’s job is to vary the problem, not correct the solution. Trust the process.
Principle 04
Robust Skill
DL-trained players perform better under pressure and in novel conditions because their skill is built on adaptability, not a single practised pattern.
🎯 Practice Design
Explore & Exploit
Explore & Exploit is a practice design concept drawn from motor learning and ecological dynamics. It describes two distinct modes of practice that serve different purposes and should both exist in a well-designed training programme.

Exploration is open-ended discovery. Players are given freedom to find solutions, make mistakes, and develop understanding through experience. There are no wrong answers — only attempts. The constraint is loose, the space is generous, the rule is minimal. This is where players build their solution space.

Exploitation is applying what has been found under pressure and specificity. The constraint is tight, the numbers are realistic, the condition mirrors the game. Players are now executing a solution they understand — being tested on it rather than discovering it.

The danger of only exploiting: players who never explore become brittle — they can execute their rehearsed pattern but fail when the game presents something new. The danger of only exploring: players develop understanding but lack sharpness under pressure.

Good session design moves players from explore to exploit across the learning loop — or deliberately sits in one mode based on where the team is in the cycle.
Mode 01
Explore
Open-ended. Low constraint. Multiple solutions accepted. Players discover through trial. Coach observes more than intervenes. Early-phase or early-season emphasis.
Mode 02
Exploit
Tight constraint. Game-specific numbers and space. Players execute known solutions under pressure. Coach enforces principles. In-season or pre-match emphasis.
Application
Sequencing
Explore in Phases 01–02 of a session, exploit in Phases 03–04. Or build across a week: explore early in the week, exploit closer to match day.
Red Flag
Always Exploiting
If players only ever drill the same patterns, they stop learning. When the game presents something new they have no tools. Build the solution space first.
📋 Practice Design
The Importance of Reflection
Reflection is the mechanism by which experience becomes learning. Without structured reflection — by both players and coaches — even the best-designed practice produces only physical adaptation. The cognitive and tactical learning loop stays incomplete.

Player reflection develops self-awareness, ownership, and the ability to transfer learning independently. When players evaluate their own performance — rather than waiting to be told what they did — they develop as decision-makers, not just skilled movers.

Coach reflection drives session design improvement. The question is not just “did they do it?” but “did the constraint produce the behaviour? Did the design create the problem I intended?” Coaches who reflect on their own design get better at designing.

Reflection doesn’t require long team meetings. One sharp question at the right moment is more valuable than a ten-minute debrief. The Set Attack session’s whiteboard principle is a reflection tool: players generate the principles, evaluate them at the end, and own the outcome.

The goal is a player who leaves the pitch having understood what happened — not just one who was told.
Type 01
In-Session
Brief freeze during a game, one question, players answer, play resumes. 30 seconds. Connects experience directly to principle while the feeling is live.
Type 02
Post-Session
One or two questions at the end. Player-led. Coach confirms, challenges, or clarifies — doesn’t lead. The player speaks first, always.
Type 03
Coach Reflection
Did the constraint produce the intended behaviour? Was the space right? Did the game afford what I planned? Honest answers improve the next session design.
Principle
Player Speaks First
The player who evaluates their own performance owns their development. Ask before you tell. Wait. Let the silence work. Then confirm or redirect.
The Learning Loop Closes Here
Every session in this hub is designed to connect back to match performance. Reflection is how the loop closes — turning practice experience into game understanding. The player who understands why will always outperform the player who was just told what.
🎯 Design Tactical Guidelines
Designing Problems, Not Giving Solutions
The most powerful shift a coach can make is from solution-giver to problem-designer. When a coach tells a player what to do, the player executes. When a coach designs a problem that invites the right behaviour, the player discovers it — and owns it far more deeply.

This section draws on design tactical guidelines developed by Mark O’Sullivan. The two frameworks below offer a practical toolkit for designing games that connect to the real problems players face in match conditions.
Framework 01 — Task Design Model
Ball · Opponent(s) · Direction · Consequence
Task design shapes what players perceive and what they are compelled to do. Four elements are the foundations of any well-designed game. The key information in any practice should be representative of the game — if it isn’t, players are solving the wrong problem.
Element 01
Ball
The ball is the primary information source. Its position, trajectory, and possession status drives every decision. Practice must keep the ball central — not as a prop, but as the key stimulus.
Element 02
Opponent(s)
Opponents create the problem. Their positioning, pressure, and intentions force decisions. Without realistic opposition, players are solving easier problems than the game presents. Co-adaptation of attack and defence is key.
Element 03
Direction
Games have direction — an end-goal to attack and one to defend. Directionality creates the defensive and offensive tensions of the real game. Remove direction and you remove half the tactical problem.
Element 04
Consequence
Consequence creates stakes. If we lose the ball and don’t win it back, the opponent may score. This continuity of attack and defence — and the cost of losing possession — must be present in practice design.
Framework 02 — Design Principles
Through · Around · Over
Before designing any game or practice, a coach should ask one simple question: “Will this game invite players to go through, around, or over the opposition?” The intention shapes the design. Keep it simple. Have clear intentions. The game must afford the behaviour you want to develop.
Intention 01
Through
Playing through the opposition — penetrating defensive lines, exploiting space between defenders, running directly at the press. Games designed for this will have directional overloads, tight spaces, and high turnover consequence.
Intention 02
Around
Moving the ball around the opposition — switching, wide play, recycling, finding the far side. Games designed for this will reward patience, width, and ball movement that shifts the defensive shape before committing.
Intention 03
Over
Playing over the top — high ball delivery, aerial contests, playing in behind. Games designed for this will have deep defensive setups, target players, and contested aerial zones. Requires direction and consequence to be meaningful.
Design Rule
Keep It Simple
One clear intention per game. Know what you want the game to invite before you set it up. If you can’t state the intention in one sentence, the design isn’t clear enough yet.

🎯 Practice Design Method
Constrain to Afford
The word afford comes from James Gibson’s ecological psychology — an affordance is a possibility for action that an environment makes available. In sport, when you change a rule, alter the numbers, or restructure the space, you don’t just change what players can do — you change what they naturally want to do.

That’s the mechanism. You’re not telling them what to do. You’re making the right action the most available one.
The Key Distinction
Traditional coaching
Info TO the player
Instruction, feedback, demonstration. The coach is the source. The player executes what they’re told.
Constrain to Afford
Info INTO the environment
The player reads the situation and responds. The coach designs the problem — not the solution.
The question changes from “How do I get them to do this?” to “What constraint would make this the obvious thing to do?”
The Three Levers — and What Each Affords
Lever 01
Task Constraints
Rules · scoring structures · time windows · problems given to the group
A 15-second window to score in the central lane or between the posts is a task constraint — it’s a problem given to the attackers to solve, not a skill limit imposed on them. The game still runs; the time window is the challenge. This is a classic Explore → Exploit bridge: players first discover ways to get the ball forward quickly, then learn to exploit the patterns that work under pressure.

A bonus point for a score created from wide is another task constraint. The game is unchanged but the reward structure now affords wide play. Players seek width naturally because the environment rewards it — not because they were told to.

These constraints don’t limit skill. They create problems that skill is required to solve.
Lever 02
Environmental Constraints
Space · numbers · lanes · zones · goals · starting positions
Constraining the defence to keep a set number of players in the central lane affords overloads and space on the wide channels — without telling attackers to go wide. The environment produces the opportunity; the attacker reads and responds.

A numerical overload (3v2, 4v3) affords attacking confidence and forces defenders into prioritisation decisions. Removing the goalkeeper affords finishing in front of goal without the psychological barrier of a keeper.

Asking a defender to hold a specific starting position before engaging — such as on the arc before every play — is an environmental constraint that affords timing and space advantages for attackers while giving the defender their own problem to solve.
Lever 03
Individual Constraints
Roles · areas of responsibility · starting positions · physical capacity
A player constrained to a specific zone, role, or starting position is not being limited — they’re being given a focused problem. A physically dominant player afforded more 1v1 situations develops that quality through the constraint of repeated exposure. A player who tends to drift centrally, constrained to wide channels, must develop new movement and decision-making patterns. The constraint is individual; the adaptation is specific.
Tactical C2A — Two Directions
At the tactical level, Constrain to Afford works in two directions. The choice depends on which unit you want to develop and which unit you want to use as the constraint.
Direction 01
Constrain the Defence
Set the defence as man-to-man, zonal, or holding a specific line. Watch what the attackers find and create in response. The defensive structure is the constraint; the attack has the problem to solve.
Direction 02
Constrain the Attack
Tell the attack they must go through, around, or over. Watch how the defence reads, organises, and adapts in response. The attacking intention is the constraint; the defence has the problem to solve.
Practical Examples
Example 01
Playing Through the Press
Three constraints that each afford play through the press — not because players are told to, but because each one makes penetration the natural response.
Constraint A
Man-to-man — tackle your own man only
Defenders are locked to their player. Attackers have a clear read on who they’re facing. Off-ball movement to create angles becomes the natural solution because the structure makes space predictable and exploitable.
Constraint B
Score in middle lane only · within 20 / 18 / 15 seconds
The lane constraint affords central penetration. The time window builds urgency and escalates as players develop. Attackers must find the route through the press, not around it. A classic Explore → Exploit progression.
Constraint C — Higher Challenge
Defenders must hit a line before engaging
Defenders start on the arc and must touch either the endline or the halfway line before play engages. Attackers receive from the defender’s kick and attack immediately. Deliberately chaotic — disrupts defensive structure, creates space, and forces attackers to read and react quickly. Best applied once base patterns are established.

Example 02
Defenders Holding Shape — Separation Game
The Separation Game creates the structural constraint. But the constraint alone is incomplete without a principle to organise it.
The Constraint
Man-to-man · tackle your own man only
Every restart stopped until man-to-man is fully set. Defenders are responsible only for their own player. This affords positional discipline and forces defenders to track movement without chasing the ball.
📋 Sub-Principle
“Meet Them When They Come In”
A defender does not follow their attacker everywhere. If the attacker drifts into a non-dangerous area far from the ball, the defender stays compact and monitors. When the attacker moves toward danger — toward the ball, toward the goal, toward an area of threat — the defender engages.

This sub-principle works with the constraint. Without it, defenders chase every run and lose their shape. With it, they develop the skill of ball and man awareness simultaneously — scanning regularly, protecting the danger zone in front of goal, and making the decision about when to leave off and when to engage.
What This Develops
Scanning · Positional discipline · Ball & man awareness · Danger zone protection · Decision-making about when to engage

The Design Questions
Before setting up any practice, a coach using C2A asks:
01
What behaviour do I want to see more of?
02
What constraint would make that behaviour the path of least resistance?
03
What does this game currently afford — and is that what I want?
04
What unintended behaviour might this constraint invite?
What to Avoid
Avoid
Over-Constraining
Stacking too many rules creates confusion rather than clarity. One clear constraint per game. More constraints = less information available to the player.
Avoid
Constraints as Punishment
A constraint should invite a behaviour, not penalise failure. Sitting out for losing the ball is discipline, not design. The two are different tools for different purposes.
Avoid
Not Observing
The constraint generates information about your players and your design. If you’re not watching, you’re wasting the practice. Observe before you intervene.
📌 On Time-Based Scoring Constraints
It is valid to constrain around scoring — scoring is the point of the game. But there is an important distinction in how you apply time pressure:

A hard cutoff (“score in 10 seconds or the ball is dead”) asks for very direct and usually difficult solutions. This can be useful for high-level teams or as a competitive challenge between groups. It tends to produce urgent, decisive play — useful stimulus at the right moment.

A bonus point for scoring within a time window — while the game continues regardless — is a softer version of the same constraint. Players are incentivised toward speed without being forced into poor decisions. Both are valid C2A. The key is knowing which you are using and why. The bonus model invites the behaviour. The hard cutoff demands it.
Progression Logic
Explore
Loose constraint, many solutions, player-led discovery. The practice affords exploration of the problem. Coach observes. Phases 01–02.
Direct
Tighter constraint, fewer solutions, the desired behaviour becomes prominent. The practice affords a specific response. Coach names what they see. Phase 03.
Challenge
Game-realistic constraint. The behaviour must now work under opposition pressure and fatigue. This is Phase 04 — the tactical conditioned game. The constraint is the match itself.

🏆 Ecological Dynamics — Game Model Design
Building a Game Model in an Ecological Way
A game model in the ecological dynamics framework is not a playbook. It is a flexible scaffold — a set of principles, intentions, and environmental conditions that guide how players and teams adapt to the ever-changing demands of the game. Players and teams co-adapt to the game environment rather than executing predetermined patterns.

The shift is from prescribing what to do to designing conditions in which the right behaviours emerge.
Two Approaches to the Game Model
Traditional
Playbook Model
Reductionist & deterministic approaches
Prescriptive & predetermined — fixed patterns of play
Coach-led directives — players comply
Simplified environments — rehearsal & repetition
Automatisation — limited adaptability
Reduced exposure to complexity — constrained decision-making
Ecological
Adaptive Model
Flexible scaffold — not a blueprint to follow
Adaptive & emergent — shaped through interaction
Player-environment interaction — perception-action coupling
Representative learning — rich, information-dense practice
Decision-making — exploration & problem solving
Exposure to informational complexity — flexible, context-sensitive behaviour
The Shift
From Playbook
to Adaptive Performance
Players and teams co-adapt to the ever-changing game environment. The model does not prescribe what will happen — it creates the conditions in which the right things can happen.
The Game as a Continuous Flow
Football is not a sequence of separate phases — it is a continuous, evolving system with augmented information flowing across all moments of the game.
The Transition Nexus
“The Transition Nexus is where the game is most fragile and most creative. It is the moment of attunement, recalibration and reorganisation where perception, intention and action converge.”
Jones et al., 2026
What This Means for Coaches
01
Design Environments, Not Instructions
Create conditions that afford the right behaviours. The practice design does the teaching — not the instruction.
02
Guide Attention, Not Movements
Use tactical principles as attentional anchors. Direct where players look and what they notice — the movement follows.
03
Use Feedback That Informs Decisions
Questioning, prompts, and images over telling. Ask before you tell. Let players evaluate before the coach delivers a verdict.
04
Embrace Variability and Emergence
Promote exploration, adaptation and co-adaptation. The unpredictable moment in practice is where the learning lives — not something to be eliminated.
05
Develop Players, Not Just Patterns
Build perception, decision-making and adaptability. A player who understands the game will always outperform a player who only knows their role in a pattern.
Tactical Principles as Attentional Anchors
Principles don’t prescribe movement — they direct attention. A player anchored to a principle will find their own solution within it.
Anchor 01
Perception
See the information that matters. The first skill is knowing what to look at before deciding what to do. Tactical principles focus the perceptual search.
Anchor 02
Decision-Making
Select viable options. Not the perfect option — a viable one. Decision-making under pressure improves through exposure to representative problems in practice.
Anchor 03
Action
Execute with intent. A good decision executed with commitment is more effective than a better decision executed hesitantly. Intention is part of the action.
Anchor 04
Co-Adaptation
Interact and influence each other. Players co-adapt in real time — the attacker shapes the defender, the defender shapes the attacker. This is the game.
Anchor 05
Emergence
Adaptive behaviours emerge from the system. You cannot prescribe every solution — you create the conditions and let the behaviour emerge from the interaction.
Anchor 06
Principles
Tactical principles as affordance-based intentions. A principle tells players what to look for and what it affords — not where to stand or what to do.

⚡ The Competitive Mindset Framework
Attention · Intention · Intensity
These three qualities sit beneath everything that happens in competition and in practice. They are not tactics or techniques — they are the mental architecture that determines how a player engages with the game. Great competitors train all three, and great coaches design for all three.
Quality 01
Attention
The Bedrock of Mindset
The most important mental skill you have. Great competitors learn over days, weeks, and years to amplify and flex their attention. They go again, refusing to give in to distraction. They go again, determined to stay connected to the game.
Teammates
An attention trained towards your teammates — reading movement, anticipating needs, staying connected.
Opposition
An attention homing in on the opposition — their shape, their cues, their intentions.
Space & Ball
An attention divided between space and ball — the dual scan that separates the perceptive from the reactive.
Game Plan
An attention directed towards game plans, responsibilities and actions — the collective intention held in mind.
Coaching Application
The coach draws attention to what is important — through analysis, feedback, reflection and practice design. Every coaching decision is a choice about where attention goes.
Quality 02
Intention
A Mind-Body State
Constantly influencing the style in which you execute your actions. Great competitors feel the direction of their energy through the experience of being in their bodies — and they do all they can to position it forward.
Purposeful
Purposeful with ambition — positive with movements — proactive with gameplay. Embracing the opportunities and attacking the dangers.
Free
Free from inhibition. Shrugging off feelings of passiveness. Refusing the limitations of hesitancy.
Forward
Competing with a high level of intent — energy-forward. In control and in charge.
📋 Shared Intentionality
“As a team we share an intention”
In a practice sense, intention works best when players and coaches come to a shared idea of what the intention is — now, in the near future, and for the big picture. This is shared intentionality. It operates in a micro sense (this moment, this play) and a macro sense (this season, this team).
Quality 03
Intensity
A Psychological Ferocity
The mental engagement to accompany physical effort. Great competitors exhibit an intensity that is impervious to goals and scores against, to mistakes made and moments of brilliance from hard-worn opponents. They go again with an optimal level of intensity: alert and ready.
The Optimal Zone
Too Low
Optimal
Too High
Refuse to drop too low
Lethargy — complacency — despondency. Giving in to the feeling that the moment doesn’t matter.
Refuse to rise too high
Anxiety — anger — frustration. Giving in to the feeling that the moment is too much.
Coaching Application
Intensity is at the crossover of clever practice design and the player’s desire to bring it. The coach creates the conditions — the competitive stakes, the standards, the culture of the session — and the player chooses to meet them.
Why All Three Matter Together
Attention
without Intention is scattered focus
Intention
without Intensity is direction without drive
Intensity
without Attention is effort without wisdom
All three working together creates the competitor who is connected to the game, positioned forward, and operating at the right pitch — alert and ready, every time.
